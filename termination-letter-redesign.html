Termination Letter Redesign

As someone who has been recently left their job, I need to know what to do with my old 401(k) so that I am prepared for retirement.

April 2020

Contributors

Aaron Pfannebaker (content strategy) and a cross-functional team of developers and product managers. Foundational research conducted by Tatiana Kligerman and Daniel Orbach.

Tools/Approach

Tools/Techniques: Interviews, Moderated Testing, Iterative design, InDesign, Agile, Lean UX

Discovery

The opportunity

Reimagine a standardized letter that gets delivered within 80 days of termination to all customers who have recently left their jobs to ultimately increase assets retained and rolled over to Prudential.

The Impact

The letter comes at a key point where we have the ability to guide a customer in a time of uncertainty. Directing calls to our retirement counselors could increase the number of assets kept in Prudential plans and rolled over into one of our IRA solutions.

The Users

Troy, 60, Freelance technician

140K in Savings | 200K in Retirement Assets | $80,000K in Annual Income
After a successful career of 20 years in manufacturing, Troy decided to strike out on his own as a freelance technician. Now that he has more clients, he hasn’t had the time to think about his multiple 401(k) accounts. Turning 60 this year has gotten him started thinking about what he should be doing to prepare for retirement.

“I’ve been contributing to my 401K for years but now that i’m getting close to retirement, I really want to make sure i’m doing the right stuff to get me to where I need to be.”
- Troy

Motivations

Troy wants to feel comfortable that they are prepared to retire in a few years

02

Troy wants to maintain their comfortable standard of living for them and their partner throughout retirement

03

Troy wants to have everything in order so he doesn't have to worry when it comes time to start withdrawing his retirement funds.

Testing
and
Validation

Test. Design. Test again. Design. With rapid one-week sprints, I tested every single iteration of our design with customers and Retirement Counselors. I facilitated workshops, conducted moderated testing, and synthesized results as a part of every sprint. Keeping in lock-step with our customers was an essential part of the development process at every step of the way.

ADDITIONAL CONTENT I TESTED

Findings

01

When it came to retirement, customers didn't know where to begin. They knew retirement was important but wanted advice on how to best save up for it.

02

Customers has difficulty understanding terms like “Roth,”  “Rollover Dollars,” and “Vested balance.”  

03

Customers were interested in calling into our Retirement Counselors, but didn’t know what to discuss or ask for advice about.  

The Solution

Our revised letter contained the following:

  • Approachable and customer-friendly languge that comforted customers in a time of stress and concern
  • A glossary of definitions to help customers decipher complicated financial jargon
  • A list of proactive questions to ask our counselors when they decided to call in to better set the tone for a productive conversation.

Looking Forward

Although the end product was a physical letter, this project was an incredible opportunity to see how transparency, collaboration, and a prescriptive agile process could be applied to anything. The next steps were to expand to an omni-channel experience where we follow through from the letter to a customized online experience.

Learnings

01

Transparency does not mean visibility, it means shared understanding. Frequently sharing work and informally gathering feedback invited thoughts that could never be formed in isolation.

02

Genuine and willing collaboration is at the foundation of any successful project. Working on a scrum team towards one common objective each sprint ensured alignment (and camradarie) at every step of the way.

03

Design work is best when shared. Some of the best ideas not only came from our customers, but non-designers alike. Working together on research, flows, and design ideas was a great way to hear from people with different perspectives.
